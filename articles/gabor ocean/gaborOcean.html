A Gentle Introduction To Gabor Noise

Noise and The Frequency Spectrum (WIP)

A common way to analyse the properties of different types of noise and of images in general is to look at its Fourier transform. The Fourier transform takes in an input signal and reveals the frequencies present within the signal by decomposing it into sine and cosine pairs. This makes it easier to quantify various properties of different noises.

FIGURE.

Specifically, we look at the power spectrum or the squared magnitude of the complex sinusoid resulting from the Fourier transform. In two dimensions this can be represented as a polar graph where the coordinates \((r, \theta)\) represent the frequency and direction of the corresponding 2D plane wave, respectively.

FIGURE.

Gabor noise is unique in that it allows for precise control over the power spectrum of the resulting noise. The fundamental primitive of Gabor noise is the Gabor kernel, a function whose parameters have a direct correspondence to the shape of the kernel in the frequency domain.

FIGURE.

This suggests that, given the power spectrum of some exemplar texture, we can reproduce variations of that texture simply by generating Gabor noise with the appropriate parameters. Determining these parameters has been the subject of many papers within the field.

Sparse Convolution

Gabor noise is really an extension to a technique sometimes referred to as sparse convolution. To explain sparse convolution we first need to take a look at plain old white noise. The Fourier transform of white noise is simply just white noise again. This makes sense since white noise by definition contains all frequencies.

FIGURE.

Now consider convolving white noise with an arbitrary signal. Since convolution in the spatial domain is equivalent to multiplication in the frequency domain, the result is the Fourier transform of the signal multiplied by white noise.

FIGURE.

Sparse convolution aims to exploit this behaviour by convolving the signal with a small number of discrete samples. The samples' values and locations are randomly distributed such that as the number of samples increases the result converges towards regular white noise. It turns out that it doesn't take too many samples to acceptably approximate white noise.

FIGURE.

This is a nice property as it allows us to directly control the frequency content of procedural noise while remaining entirely in the spatial domain. We can achieve sparse convolution by summing up copies of our input signal distributed randomly in space with varying amplitudes.

FIGURE.

Now back to Gabor noise. Recall that all the parameters defining a Gabor kernel have a direct correspondence to its appearance in the frequency domain. By taking Gabor kernels as our input signal during sparse convolution, we can in theory produce arbitrary textures with complete control over their spectral content.

FIGURE.

Optimising Gabor Noise (WIP)

If we were to implement Gabor noise via sparse convolution as-is the resulting algorithm would have a complexity of \(O(NM)\), where \(N\) and \(M\) are the number of pixels and the number of kernel samples evaluated, respectively. Note however that the Gaussian window approaches zero quickly such that its contribution to pixels that lie beyond a few standard deviations of its mean becomes neglibable. Instead we can truncate the Gaussian past a certain distance, or even better, choose a different window altogether that approximates a Gaussian but has finite support.

FIGURE.

For a given effective kernel radius \(r\), we can stratify the domain into cells of size \(r \times r\), and instead evaluate the \(3 \times 3\) grid of cells around a given pixel. This reduces the complexity to \(O(9N)\).

FIGURE.

We can optimise this even more if we fix the windows on a lattice and instead randomise the phase of the Gabor kernel to break up the regularity of the lattice. Then we only need to evaluate \(2 \times 2\) lattice points per pixel, just as we do with Perlin noise.

FIGURE.

Finally, we can take this one step further by borrowing from Simplex noise and stratifying the domain into simplices such that each pixel only needs to evaluate its \(3\) local lattice points. The simplical grid also helps reduce rectilinear artifacts.

FIGURE.

Getting Dirty with the Details

The Unique Spectral Properties of Gabor Kernels

Classic Gabor

The parameters defining a Gabor kernel have a direct correspondence to the parameters of a Gaussian in the spectral domain. First, let's take a look at the sinusoidal component. Consider a plane wave with wavevector \(\mathbf{k}\), phase \(\varphi\), and amplitude \(A\):

$$ f(\mathbf{x}; \mathbf{k}, \phi, A) = A \sin(\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{x} + \varphi) $$

The magnitude of the Fourier transform

$$ |\mathcal{F}(f(\mathbf{x}))(\mathbf{\xi})| = A \delta(\mathbf{\xi} \pm \mathbf{k}) $$

Next, consider a Gaussian with covariance matrix \(\mathbf{\Sigma}\):

$$ g(\mathbf{x}; \mathbf{\Sigma}) = \exp(-\mathbf{x}^{\top}\mathbf{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{x}) $$

Likewise,

$$ |\mathcal{F}(g(\mathbf{x}))(\mathbf{\xi})| = \dfrac{\pi}{\sqrt{|\mathbf{\Sigma}^{-1}|}} \exp(-\dfrac{1}{4}\mathbf{\xi}^{\top}\mathbf{\Sigma}\mathbf{\xi}) $$

All that's left is to find the Fourier transform of the product \(f(\mathbf{x})g(\mathbf{x})\). The convolution theorem states that this is equivalent to \(\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) * \hat{g}(\mathbf{x})\). Since \(\hat{f}\) is just a scaled and shifted the dirac delta, the sifting property of the Fourier transform tells us that the result is simply an equivalently scaled and shifted \(\hat{g}\). Which leads us to the final formulation:

$$ \hat{G}(\mathbf{\xi}; \mathbf{k}, \phi, A, \mathbf{\Sigma}) = \dfrac{A\pi}{\sqrt{|\mathbf{\Sigma}^{-1}|}} \exp(-\dfrac{1}{4} (\mathbf{\xi} \pm \mathbf{k})^{\top}\mathbf{\Sigma}(\mathbf{\xi} \pm \mathbf{k})) $$

So given the parameters of a frequency-domain Gaussian \(\hat{\mathbf{\Sigma}}\), \(\hat{\mathbf{\mu}}\), \(\hat{A}\), the parameters of the corresponding spatial-domain Gabor kernel are:

$$ \mathbf{k} = \mathbf{\hat{\mu}}, \quad A = \dfrac{\hat{A} \sqrt{|\hat{\mathbf{\Sigma}}^{-1}}|}{\pi}, \quad \mathbf{\Sigma} = 4 \hat{\mathbf{\Sigma}}^{-1} $$

The Window Function

As mentioned earlier we'd like to replace the Gaussian window with a different window that has finite support and which closely approximates the frequency response of a Gaussian. A good candidate would be the Kaiser window. However, the window is not simple to evaluate as it involves non-elementary functions. Instead I propose a window with similar properties but which is far simpler to evaluate. It is derived from the limit definition of the exponential function:

$$ e^{x} = \lim_{\alpha \to \infty} \left(1 + \dfrac{x}{\alpha}\right)^{\alpha} $$

From this we can express a Gaussian in the form \(e^{-ax^2}\) as the following limit:

$$ e^{-ax^2} = \lim_{\alpha \to \infty} (1 - x^2)^{\alpha} $$

Thus we have a window defined as:

$$ w(x) = (1 - x^2)^{\alpha}, \quad -1 \lt x \le 1 $$

If \(\alpha\) is an integer, then the window can be evaluated using only a handful of multiply and add instructions. In practice a value of \(\alpha = 3\) produces visually accurate results, and has the necessary \(C^2\) continuity to prevent artifacts in reflections when rendering the surface.

The \(I\) term is a normalisation constant that ensures the total area under the window is equal to one. It is defined as:

$$ I = \int_{\mathbb{R}} w(\mathbf{x}) \, d\mathbf{x} $$

In \(\mathbb{R}^2\), the integral has a simple form:

$$ I = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} w(\mathbf{x}) \, d\mathbf{x} = 2\pi \int_0^1 x(1 - x^2)^{\alpha} \, dx = \dfrac{\pi}{\alpha + 1} $$

Care needs to be taken when altering the window of the Gabor kernel to ensure that the conversion of parameters from the frquency domain to the spatial domain and vice versa remains correct. In general, for a windowing function \(w(x)\) the amplitude of the frequency response is equal to the integral of the window over the entire domain:

$$ \hat{A} = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} w(\mathbf{x})\, d\mathbf{x} $$

The scaling property of the Fourier transform tells us how the frequency response changes when the window is scaled. For a positive scaling matrix \(\mathbf{S}\), the frequency response is:

$$ \dfrac{1}{|\mathbf{S}|} \hat{w}(\mathbf{S}^{-1}\xi) $$

Simulating Wave Spectra (WIP)

Spectral Sampling

A common technique in ocean simulation is to take a parameterised description of the ocean in the frequency domain and transform it to the spatial domain via the discrete Fourier transform. The power of Gabor noise however is that it gives us precise control of our noise's spectral content while remaining entirely in the spatial domain. Recall that the bandwidth of a Gabor kernel is inversely proportional to the spatial width of its window. When generating Gabor noise on a fixed lattice, all windows have the same width. This means that the final generated noise is band-limited.

FIGURE

By summing multiple bands of noise at different frequencies and amplitudes we can easily generate arbitrary isotropic spectra. This is exactly the process behind the popular fractional Brownian Motion (fBM) technique. Given a description of some spectral profile as a function \(s(k)\) that takes in a frequency \(k\) and returns a magnitude \(|z|\), we can shape our noise by feeding the center frequency of each band through the function to determine the corresponding spatial-domain amplitude of that band.

FIGURE

We can reframe this as a sampling problem. We want to reconstruct the spectral profile by taking the sum of \(N\) discrete samples (bands) at sample points (frequencies) \(x_n\) using a Gaussian kernel \(f(x)\) of width (bandwidth) \(B_n\). Formally:

$$ s(k) \approx \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} f(\dfrac{x_n - k}{B_n})s(x_n) $$

If our spectral profile \(s(k)\) is unchanging, then we could formulate this as a minimisation problem to find the optimal \(k_n\), \(B_n\) for some fixed \(N\). However, a more generic approach is preferred as it allows for real-time flexibility. The simplest solution is just to sample \(s(k)\) at fixed intervals of equal bandwidth, effectively resulting in a Gaussian smoothing of the original profile.

FIGURE

There are two main drawbacks to this however. The first is that the sample rate must be high enough to capture sharp peaks in the spectrum lest important details be lost. The second is that sampling large regions of low-energy content in the spectrum leads to a waste of computation as these areas contribute very little to the final profile, and this is compounded by the first issue should the sample rate be very high.

FIGURE

Fortunately, wave spectra share similar characteristics that we can exploit to our advantage. Spectra are characterised by a sharp low-frequency peak with a gradual falloff as the frequency increases. This suggests that samples should be concentrated around the peak frequency, spreading out as we sample the higher frequencies. By increasing the sample distance \(h\) in proportion to the bandwidth we can efficiently cover the whole spectrum with much fewer samples. For a given spectral profile \(s(k)\) we need to determine the peak frequency \(f_0\).The first sample can then be placed at \(f_0\) with the bandwidth following an increasing geometric series.

FIGURE

The bandwidth of the first sample should be just big enough to approximately cover the lower tail of the spectrum. To do this, we can use the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the lower half of the peak. Finding the FWHM for arbtitrary spectra can be difficult however as an analytic solution is not always guaranteed to exist. Instead we use a 2nd-order Taylor approximation at the peak, resulting in a quadratic whose FWHM can be easily derived.

FIGURE

For a general quadratic \( ax^2 + bx + c \), the FWHM \(\gamma\) is given by the following:

$$ \gamma = \dfrac{\sqrt{4ac - b^2}}{|a|} $$

From here we seek the corresponding Gaussian with the same FWHM. For a general Gaussian of the form \( e^{-\sigma x^2} \) its FWHM is:

$$ \gamma = 2 \sqrt{\dfrac{\ln{2}}{\sigma}} $$

Rearranging for \(\sigma\):

$$ \sigma = \dfrac{4 \ln{2}}{\gamma^2} $$

We know that for a Gaussian in the frequency-domain with scaling parameter \(\sigma\), the corresponding spatial-domain parameter is \(\dfrac{1}{4\sigma}\). Thus the equation becomes:

$$ \sigma = \dfrac{\gamma^2}{8 \ln{2}} $$

From here we can find the

$$ \gamma = \dfrac{4 \sqrt{2} \ln{2}}{\gamma} $$

For a frequency domain Gaussian with bandwidth \(\sigma\), the FWHM \(\gamma\) of the corresponding spatial domain Gaussian is:

$$ \gamma = 4 \sqrt{\sigma \ln{2}} $$

We define the sample distance \(h\) as a function of the bandwidth. This is useful in general especially when attempting to produce periodic noise, where the resolution of the lattice grid and consequently the width of the spatial kernel is limited to a unit fraction of the texture size.

A suitable metric for determining the distance \(h\) between two samples is to place them such that they sum to 1 at their intersection, or equivalently:

$$ h_n = \dfrac{B_n + B_{n-1}}{2} $$

Where \(B_n\) is the bandwidth (or FWHM) of the \(n\)th Gaussian sample.

The fundamental of a spectral profile is found where \(s'(f_0) = 0\).

Directional Spreading

So far we have ignored the directional component of the ocean spectrum. This is largely because it is seperable from the isotropic component, and thus we can use alternative sampling strategies to shape it. A directional spectral profile often used ocean optics is the cosine 2s spreading function. It is defined as:

$$ D(\theta) = \dfrac{1}{\kappa} \cos(\omega - \theta)^{2s}, \quad -\dfrac{\pi}{2} \le \omega - \theta \le \dfrac{\pi}{2} \quad \kappa = \int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} \cos(\theta)^{2s} \,d\theta $$

Where \(s\) is a sharpness parameter controlling the spread of the distribution. The normalisation constant \( \dfrac{1}{\kappa} \) has an analytical expression in terms of the gamma function, but it can be more easily approximated by taking the area of a box whose width is the angular bandwidth \(\beta\) of the spreading function:

$$ \kappa \approx \beta, \quad \beta = 2 \arccos(2^{-1/2s}) $$

One method to approximate the directional spread is by altering the shape of the Gaussian perpendicular to the main axis. We'll do this by projecting the angular bandwidth \(\beta\) of the directional distribution onto a plane at the origin perpendicular to the wavevector \(\mathbf{k}\). This is achieved using basic trigonometry:

$$ B = 2k \sin(\dfrac{\beta}{2}) = 2k \sqrt{1 - 2^{-1/s}}, \quad k = |\mathbf{k}| $$

Another strategy is to randomly perturb the direction of each kernel's wavevector \(\mathbf{k}\) to match the distribution of the spreading function. For a given directional distribution \(D(\theta)\), we can approximate it with a Gaussian distribution over \(\theta\). The standard deviation \(\sigma_D\) of this Gaussian is derived from the angular bandwidth \(\beta\):

$$ \sigma_D = \dfrac{\beta}{2\sqrt{2\ln{2}}} $$

We can perturb each wavevector by a random Gaussian-distributed angular offset with zero mean and variance \(\sigma_k^2\). The combined standard deviation of the perturbed Gaussians is related to the bandwidth \(B_n\) of each Gaussian by:

$$ \sigma_D = \sigma_k + \dfrac{1}{2N\sqrt{2\ln{2}}} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} 4\arcsin(\dfrac{B_n}{4k}) $$

Another important detail in simulating accurate oceans is taking into account how the spreading function varies with the frequency of the wave. Lower frequency waves exhibit a narrower directional spread whereas high frequency waves are more isotropic.

Ocean Swell

It is not uncommon to see two peaks in ocean spectra, corresponding to waves produced by wind and as well as swell. A second peak can be simulated by adding another band of noise. The principal direction of each peak is often uncorrelated.

Wave Frequency and the Dispersion Relation

Most oceanographic spectra describe waves in terms of their angular frequency, that is the number of cycles per unit time. However, we want waves in terms of their spatial frequency or wavenumber. As such we need to be able to convert from one to the other.

Dispersion relation...

Given a frequency-domain Gaussian with parameters \(\hat{A}\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{\Sigma}}\), we know that the corresponding spatial-domain amplitude \(A\) is \(\dfrac{\hat{A} \sqrt{|\hat{\mathbf{\Sigma}}}|}{4\pi}\). The covariance matrix can be decomposed into the product of scaling matrix \(\mathbf{S}\) and rotation matrix \(\mathbf{R}\):

$$ \hat{\mathbf{\Sigma}} = \mathbf{R} \mathbf{S} \mathbf{S^\top} \mathbf{R^\top} $$

The determinant of \(\hat{\mathbf{\Sigma}}\) is invariant with respect to the rotation \(\mathbf{R}\), so the equation for the spatial-domain amplitude can be written as:

$$ A = \dfrac{\hat{A} \sqrt{|\mathbf{S}^2|}}{4\pi} $$

\(\mathbf{S^2}\) is simply a diagonal matrix whose entries are the variance of the Gaussian along each axis. The variance \(\sigma^2\) is related to the bandwidth \(B\) through the following relation:

$$ \sigma^2 = \dfrac{B^2}{8\ln{2}} $$

Letting us simplify \(\sqrt{|\mathbf{S^2}|}\) as follows:

$$ \sqrt{|\mathbf{S}^2|} = \sqrt{\sigma_x^2 \sigma_y^2} = \sigma_x \sigma_y = \dfrac{B_x B_y}{8\ln{2}} $$

Giving us the final simplified equation:

$$ A = \dfrac{\hat{A} B_x B_y}{32\pi\ln{2}} $$

Local Parameterisation

A really nice advantage of using procedural noise to model wave spectra is the ability to make local features by changing the spectrum parameters based on the position of the current evaluation point. For example, one could augment the spectrum by feeding in a wind vector from a global wind field, allowing for more complex ocean textures. Ocean currents and surface contaminants also perturb the ocean surface.

FIGURE

This technique can be extended to other bodies of water as well. Seamless transitions between oceans, shores, lakes, and rivers are possible so long as you have a means of expressing the wave spectra of these features.

Let's follow the operations required to take a screen-space coordinate \(\mathbf{s}\) and project it onto the tangent-space of a plane at \(\mathbf{q}\). We start by determining the parameters of the ray in world-space given by \(\mathbf{r_o}\) and \(\mathbf{r_d}\). For a perspective camera, only \(\mathbf{r_d}\) is dependent on \(\mathbf{s}\) while \(\mathbf{r_o}\) is a constant, and vice-versa for an orthographic camera. In the former case a view-space direction vector \(\mathbf{d}\) can be constructed as follows:

$$ \mathbf{d} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{s_x} \\ c_f \\ \mathbf{s_y} \end{bmatrix} $$

Where \(c_f\) is the focal length, or the distance from the camera's origin to the projection plane. Note that this assumes a Y-forward convention for the view-space coordinate system, other conventions can be met by swapping or negating the components of \(\mathbf{d}\). From here we can get the world-space ray direction by normalising \(\mathbf{d}\) and multiplying it by the inverse of the camera's view matrix \(\mathbf{V}\):

$$ \mathbf{r_d} = \mathbf{V}^{-1}\dfrac{\mathbf{d}}{|\mathbf{d}|} $$

Next, we need to find the point at which the ray intersects the tangent plane. Given a plane with origin \(\mathbf{P_o}\) and normal \(\mathbf{P_N},\) we know a given point along the ray parameterised by \(t\) is on the plane when:

$$ (\mathbf{r_o} + t \mathbf{r_d} - \mathbf{P_o}) \cdot \mathbf{P_N} = 0 $$

By rearranging this equation to solve for \(t\) we can determine the intersection with the plane at \(\mathbf{p}\):

$$ \mathbf{p} = \mathbf{r_o} + t \mathbf{r_d}, \quad t = \dfrac{(\mathbf{P_o} - \mathbf{r_o}) \cdot \mathbf{P_N}}{\mathbf{r_d} \cdot \mathbf{P_N}} $$

$$ \mathbf{q} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{P_T} \\ \mathbf{P_B} \end{bmatrix} (\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{P_o}) $$

$$ \Delta\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{J'} \Delta\mathbf{s} $$

To make things easier we can express the tangent-space coordinates \(\mathbf{T}\) as a composition of functions:

$$ \mathbf{T}(\mathbf{s}) = \mathbf{q} \circ \mathbf{p} \circ \mathbf{r_d} \circ \mathbf{d} \circ \mathbf{s} $$

Then, following the chain rule, the Jacobian is simply the product of each function's Jacobians evaluated at the appropriate points:

$$ \mathbf{J_T} = \mathbf{J_q}(\mathbf{p}) \cdot \mathbf{J_p}(\mathbf{r_d}) \cdot \mathbf{J_{r_d}}(\mathbf{d}) \cdot \mathbf{J_d}(\mathbf{s}) $$

Cartesian Form of a Gerstner Wave

A Gerstner wave is effectively a curtate trochoid. A classic trochoid is defined by the following paramteric equation, where \(b\) controls the sharpness of the crest:

$$ \begin{aligned} x(t) &= a t - b \sin(t) \\ y(t) &= a - b \cos(t) \end{aligned} $$

For simplicity, we will use a unit radius \(a = 1\), giving:

$$ \begin{aligned} x(t) &= t - b \sin(t) \\ y(t) &= 1 - b \cos(t) \end{aligned} $$

For generating displacement vectors along a plane this form works fine. However for ray tracing we require a description of the trochoid in the Cartesian form \(f(t) = y\). To do this we should express \(x(t)\) in terms of \(t\) and substitute it into the equation for \(y(t)\). This can be done by finding the inverse \(x(t)^{-1}\):

$$ f(t) = 1 - b \cos(x(t)^{-1}) $$

Now, if you try to invert \(t - b \sin(t)\) you might find that it is not so easy. In fact, the inverse cannot be expressed analytically in terms of elementary functions. This makes the problem a little tricker, but fortunately we aren't the first to attempt to do such a thing. In fact, a certain Johannes Kepler derived such a function in 1609 under the study of orbital mechanics, commonly written as:

$$ M = E - e \sin(E) $$

Since then, mathematicians have been coming up with clever ways to solve Kepler's Equation for \(E\). A tried and true technique is to start with a rough initial guess and apply a number of Newton-Raphson iterations until we converge to a desired level of precision. For graphics purposes, the desired level of precision is quite low indeed. Empirically, I find as few as 2 iterations can be enough to produce a perceptually accurate Gerstner wave.

Directional Lipschitz Constant of a Height Function Along a Ray (WIP)

Let \(f(\mathbf{q})\) be a height function defined over a plane at a position \(\mathbf{q}\). The 2D position \(\mathbf{q}\) is found by projecting the 3D position \(\mathbf{p}\) onto the plane. Given the plane's origin \(\mathbf{P_o}\) as well as its tangent \(\mathbf{P_T}\) and bitangent \(\mathbf{P_B}\) vectors, this is equivalent to:

$$ \mathbf{q}(\mathbf{p}) = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{P_T} \\ \mathbf{P_B} \end{bmatrix} (\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{P_o}) $$

With it's Jacobian \(\mathbf{J_q}\) given by:

$$ \mathbf{J_q}(\mathbf{p}) = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{P_T} \\ \mathbf{P_B} \end{bmatrix} $$

The composition of \(\mathbf{q}(\mathbf{p})\) with the ray equation \(\mathbf{\delta}(t) = \mathbf{r_o} + t \mathbf{r_d}\) gives us the 2D position on the plane as a function of \(t\):

$$ \mathbf{q} \circ \mathbf{\delta}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{P_T} \\ \mathbf{P_B} \end{bmatrix} (\mathbf{r_o} + t \mathbf{r_d} - \mathbf{P_o}) $$

Whose derivatives are given by:

$$ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{J_{q \circ \mathbf{\delta}}}(t) &= \mathbf{J_q}(\mathbf{\delta}(t)) \mathbf{\delta}(t)' \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{P_T} \\ \mathbf{P_B} \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{r_d} \\ \end{aligned} $$

And its derivative is found by use of the chain rule:

$$ \begin{aligned} (\mathbf{q} \circ \mathbf{\delta}(t))' &= \nabla \mathbf{q} \circ \mathbf{\delta}(t) \cdot \mathbf{\delta}(t)' \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{P_T} \\ \mathbf{P_B} \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{\delta}(t) \cdot \mathbf{r_d} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{P_T} \\ \mathbf{P_B} \end{bmatrix} (\mathbf{r_o} + t \mathbf{r_d}) \cdot \mathbf{r_d} \\ \end{aligned} $$

Now we seek the derivative of the height along the ray, \((f \circ \mathbf{q})'\). By using the chain rule, and noting that matrix multiplication is distributive over addition:

$$ \begin{aligned} (f \circ \mathbf{q})' &= (f' \circ \mathbf{q}) \cdot \mathbf{q}' \\ &= \nabla f \circ q \cdot q' \\ &= \nabla f \circ q \cdot \nabla \begin{bmatrix} \vec{P_T} \\ \vec{P_B} \end{bmatrix} (\vec{r_o} + t \vec{r_d} - \vec{P_o}) \\ &= \nabla f \circ q \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \vec{P_T} \\ \vec{P_B} \end{bmatrix} r_d \\ \end{aligned} $$

In essence this tells us that given a global Lipschitz constant \(\lambda\) over a planar height field \(f(\vec{q})\), we can derive the Lipschitz constant of the height field along the ray by multiplying the constant with the dot product between the ray direction and the plane's normal.

Scattering Approximation (WIP)

To simulate the effect of light penetrating the water surface and scattering towards the viewer, we will use the volume rendering equation. We'll have to make some rather broad assumptions and generalisations to approximate this behaviour as we don't want to run a full path-tracing simulation. The phenomenon we're interested in particular is in-scattering, which is described by the following equation:

$$ S(\mathbf{p}_0) = \int_0^a \exp(-\smallint_0^t \sigma_e(\mathbf{p}_s) \, ds)\sigma_s(\mathbf{p}_t) \int_{S^2} \phi(\mathbf{p}_t, \theta) L(\mathbf{p}_t, \omega) \, d\omega \, dt $$

Here, \(\mathbf{p}_t\) is a shorthand for the point along a ray given by the ray equation where \(\mathbf{p}_t = \mathbf{r_o} + t \mathbf{r_d}\). \(\sigma_e\) and \(\sigma_s\) are the extinction and scattering coefficients at a point on the ray, respectively. \(L\) represents the incoming light at \(\mathbf{p}_t\) from direction \(\omega\), and \(\phi\) is the phase function at \(\mathbf{p}_t\) where \(\theta\) is the angle between the ray direction and the incoming light direction. The outermost integral runs along the ray from the start of the volume at \(t=0\) to the end at \(t=b\). The last integral runs over all the directions over the sphere \(S^2\) at the current point.

The first simplification we can make is to assume that our water volume is completely homogenous throughout such that the extinction and scattering coefficients reduce to constant terms, letting us pull them out of their respective integrals. This causes the integral inside the exponential to reduce to the current length along the ray \(t\):

$$ S(\mathbf{p}_0) = \sigma_s \int_0^a \exp(-\sigma_e t) \int_{S^2} \phi(\mathbf{p}_t, \theta) L(\mathbf{p}_t, \omega) \, d\omega \, dt $$

Next, we'll get rid of the inner integral by only considering the incoming light from the sun. The sun accounts for most of the incoming light so this makes for a reasonable approximation. Further, since our volume is homogenous, the phase function \(\phi\) is constant with respect to the position \(\mathbf{p}_t\). As we're only considering a single light direction, the angle \(\theta\) is also constant, meaning we can take the phase function entirely out of the integral.

$$ S(\mathbf{p}_0) = \phi(\theta) \sigma_s \int_0^a \exp(-\sigma_e t) L(\mathbf{p}_t, \omega) \, dt $$

The \(L(\mathbf{p}_t, \omega)\) term is in fact another integral, this time over the light path through the volume from the current point on the ray \(\mathbf{p}_t\) to the light's point of entry \(\mathbf{q_t}\), which itself is dependent on \(t\). Making this explicit:

$$ S(\mathbf{p}_0) = \phi(\theta) \sigma_s \int_0^a \exp(-\sigma_e t) \exp(-\sigma_e(|\mathbf{p}_t - \mathbf{q}_t)|) \, dt $$

We can once more pull another term out of the integral, this time by applying the mean value theorem for integrals, which states:

$$ \int_a^b f(x) g(x) \, dx = g(c) \int_a^b f(x) $$

Where \(g(c)\) takes on the average value of \(g(x)\) over \([a, b]\). So, if \(L(\mathbf{p}_c, \omega)\) gives us the mean value of \(L(\mathbf{p}_t, \omega)\), then:

$$ S(\mathbf{p}_0) = L(\mathbf{p}_c, \omega) \phi(\theta) \sigma_s \int_0^t \exp(-\sigma_e t) \, dt $$

Finally, we are left with a relatively simple integral to solve with a known analytic solution. The final equation is then:

$$ S(\mathbf{p}_0) = \dfrac{1}{\sigma_e} L(\mathbf{p}_c, \omega) \phi(\theta) \sigma_s (1 - \exp(-\sigma_e t)) $$

This leaves us with two parameters which we must find in order to evaluate the approximate in-scattering at \(\mathbf{p}_0\); the total length of the ray through the water volume \(t\), and the average length of the light ray within the water volume \(L(\mathbf{p}_c, \omega)\). Without ray-marching we can only come up with an ad-hoc hueristic based on the eye and sun directions as well as the state of the ocean surface at the evaluation point.

If we model our ocean surface as a flat plane then these parameters can be easily derived. Assuming a constant ocean depth \(d\), the total ray length \(t\) follows from basic trigonometry:

$$ t = \dfrac{\cos{\theta}}{d}, \quad \cos{\theta} = \mathbf{P_n} \cdot \mathbf{r_d} $$

Where \(\mathbf{P_n}\) and \(\mathbf{r_d}\) are the normal to the ocean surface and the direction of the view ray, respectively. To find \(L(\mathbf{p}_c, \omega)\) we can use the cosine rule. Given the distance along the view ray inside the volume \(z\), the length of the light path through the volume is:

$$ l = z \sqrt{\dfrac{a^2 + t^2 - 2at \cos{\gamma}}{t^2} }, \quad \cos{\gamma} = \mathbf{\omega} \cdot \mathbf{r_d} $$

Then the mean is found by the following:

$$ \begin{aligned} L(\mathbf{p}_c, \omega) &= \dfrac{1}{t} \int_0^t z \sqrt{\dfrac{a^2 + t^2 - 2at \cos{\gamma}}{t^2} } \, dz \\ &= \dfrac{t}{2} \sqrt{\dfrac{a^2 + t^2 - 2at \cos{\gamma}}{t^2}} \end{aligned} $$
